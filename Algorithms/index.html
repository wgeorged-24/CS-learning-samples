What is an Algorithm?

You follow algorithms every day ‚Äî you just might not call them that.

üîó Connect to What You Know

Think about making your favourite food. You probably follow steps in a certain order ‚Äî maybe you learned them from a parent, or from a recipe. That's an algorithm! It's a set of instructions that, if followed correctly, gets you to the same result every time.

Key Term Algorithm

A set of step-by-step instructions designed to perform a task or solve a problem.

What Makes Something an Algorithm?

Not every set of instructions counts as an algorithm. Let's think about what makes a good one:

ü§î Think About It

Imagine you're explaining to a friend how to get to your house. What would make your directions good?

  • They need to know where to start
  • Each step needs to be in the right order
  • The instructions need to be clear enough that they don't have to keep texting you to ask "what do you mean?"

These are exactly the features of a good algorithm:

  • It has a clear goal (what you're trying to achieve)
  • It has ordered steps (the sequence matters)
  • It can be followed by anyone (or anything) without needing to ask questions

A Real Example

Here's an everyday algorithm you might recognise:

Example Making a Cup of Tea
  1. Fill the kettle with water
  2. Turn the kettle on
  3. Put a teabag in a mug
  4. Wait for the kettle to boil
  5. Pour boiling water into the mug
  6. Wait 2-3 minutes
  7. Remove the teabag
  8. Add milk if desired
ü§î Think About It

What would happen if you did step 5 before step 2? Or step 7 before step 5? The order isn't random ‚Äî each step depends on the ones before it.

Order Matters

We've said that algorithms have ordered steps. But why does order matter so much?

üîó Think About It This Way

Imagine getting dressed in the morning. You could put on a t-shirt, then a jumper, then a coat. But if you put the coat on first, then tried to put the jumper over it... it wouldn't work! The same steps in a different order give you a completely different (and probably wrong) result.

The same is true for algorithms. Even if you have all the right steps, putting them in the wrong order can:

  • Make the task impossible to complete
  • Give you the wrong result
  • Cause errors or problems

üí° When we write algorithms for computers, getting the order wrong doesn't just look silly ‚Äî it can completely break the program or cause unexpected behaviour.

The Robot Problem

Here's something important about computers: they do exactly what you tell them ‚Äî not what you meant to tell them.

üîó Think About It This Way

If you ask a friend to "grab your bag from the car," they'll probably figure out which bag you mean, where the car is, and bring it to you. They use common sense to fill in the gaps.

Computers don't have common sense. They're more like a very literal robot that does exactly what you say ‚Äî nothing more, nothing less. If your instructions have gaps or could mean more than one thing, the computer will either do the wrong thing or get stuck.

Key Term Ambiguous

Having more than one possible meaning. Ambiguous instructions are a problem because different people (or computers) might interpret them differently.

The Three Building Blocks

Here's something remarkable: every algorithm ever written ‚Äî from a simple recipe to the code running your favourite game ‚Äî is built from just three simple ideas.

üîó Think About It This Way

It's like LEGO. With just a few types of bricks, you can build almost anything. Algorithms work the same way ‚Äî three building blocks, endless possibilities.

‚û°Ô∏è
Sequence

Steps that happen one after another, in order.

üîÄ
Selection

Making a choice based on a condition (IF something is true, THEN do this).

Repeating steps until something happens (also called a loop).

Let's Break These Down

Key Term Sequence

Instructions that are followed one after another, in order. This is the simplest building block ‚Äî just "do this, then do that, then do the next thing."

Example Sequence in Real Life

When you cross a road: Stop at the kerb ‚Üí Look left ‚Üí Look right ‚Üí Look left again ‚Üí Cross if clear. Each step happens in order, one after another.

Key Term Selection

Making a decision based on whether something is true or false. We often write this as "IF... THEN..." ‚Äî if a condition is met, then do something.

Example Selection in Real Life

IF it is raining, THEN take an umbrella. You're not always taking an umbrella ‚Äî you make a choice based on the weather.

Key Term Iteration

Repeating one or more steps. The word "iterate" means "to repeat." We often write this as "REPEAT... UNTIL..." or "WHILE... DO...". Also called a loop.

Example Iteration in Real Life

Keep rolling the dice UNTIL you get a 6. You repeat the same action (rolling) until a condition is met (getting a 6).

Key Terms

  • Algorithm ‚Äî A set of step-by-step instructions designed to complete a task or solve a problem.
  • Ambiguous ‚Äî Having more than one possible meaning. Algorithms should NOT be ambiguous.
  • Sequence ‚Äî Steps that happen one after another, in order.
  • Selection ‚Äî Making a choice based on a condition (IF... THEN...).
  • Iteration ‚Äî Repeating steps until something happens. Also called a loop.

Key Points

  • Algorithms have a clear goal and ordered steps
  • The order of steps matters ‚Äî wrong order can break the algorithm
  • Computers follow instructions exactly ‚Äî they can't guess what you meant
  • Good algorithms are precise and unambiguous
  • All algorithms use sequence, selection, and/or iteration

Examples of Everyday Algorithms

  • Recipes (making tea, baking a cake)
  • Directions to a location
  • Assembly instructions (building furniture)
  • Morning routines (getting ready for school)
